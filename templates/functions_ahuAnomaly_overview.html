AHU Anomaly

The AHU anomaly function detects common hard and soft faults related to a building's air handling unit(s).

Overview

Large commercial buildings commonly rely on air handling units (AHUs) to supply, exhaust, recirculate, and condition indoor air. These units, typically located on the roof, are an integral component in a building's heating, ventilation, and air condition (HVAC) system, and contain a number of moving mechanical components such as dampers, valves, and fans. However, with such complex arrays of actuators and sensors, all working in harmony to maintain a healthy and desirable indoor climate, it is not uncommon for faults to arise. These faults can occur from broken or deteriorating components and sensors within the AHUs, or can even be the result of a conflict in the controller logic. If left unaddressed, which they often are, these faults can result in suboptimal operation of the AHUs, leading to excessive energy consumption or even undesirable indoor climates.

The AHU anomaly function serves to identify common hard and soft faults related to AHUs. Common hard faults include stuck (open/closed) outdoor air dampers which regulate the intake of outdoor air into a building, and stuck (open/closed) heating and cooling coil valves which condition the air supplied to the building. The three common soft faults are excessive use of perimeter heating devices (baseboard heaters, VAV reheat coils, etc.) during the AHUs' economizer mode, inappropriate economizer mode settings, and the absence of a weekly schedule. More information about these common faults and how to address them is found in the outputs section.

To help identify faults, three sets of visuals are generated. The first visual plots the warmest, coldest, and average zone air temperature as a function of outdoor air. The second visual is a split-range AHU controller diagram depicting damper and valve positions as a function of outdoor air temperature. The third visual is a set of four to six simplified AHU schematics depicting the common combination of damper and valve positions and air temperatures within the AHU. More information about these visuals and how to interpret them is found in the outputs section.

Actively in development, an open-source project by the Data-driven Building Operation and Maintenance team within Carleton University's Building Performance Research Center.
